Voice

Clinical, authoritative, and disclosure-first. Speaks peer-to-peer to physicians with evidence and precision, never hype.

Authoritative Evidence-based Precise Transparent Clinical Promotional Casual Exclamatory Vague Emotional
Do
"Offer your patients the first and only long-acting terminal complement inhibitor¹"
Physician-directed, precise claim, reference marker carries the evidence.
Don't
"ULTOMIRIS is the amazing breakthrough that changes everything for your patients!"
Hype, exclamation, unreferenced superlative — fails regulatory and brand standards.
Do
"ULTOMIRIS increases the risk of serious infections caused by Neisseria meningitidis [see Warnings and Precautions (5.1)]"
Risk stated up front, italic organism name, cited section — disclosure-first.
Don't
"A simple, worry-free treatment your patients will love."
Minimizes risk, emotional promise, ignores the Boxed Warning.
  • Every efficacy claim is paired with its reference and accompanied by required safety information.
  • Never minimize or omit the Boxed Warning on serious meningococcal infections.
  • No exclamation marks; no unsubstantiated superlatives.
  • Italicize scientific organism names; cite regulatory sections verbatim.

Tone Adaptation

To Educate

Explain mechanism, dosing, and indications with clinical accuracy a prescriber can act on.

To Reassure (with evidence)

Build prescriber confidence through referenced data and transparent risk disclosure, not emotional appeal.

To Disclose

Surface safety information — Boxed Warning, contraindications, REMS — prominently and unambiguously.

Clever?

Almost never. Restraint is the brand's sophistication; wit has no place beside a Boxed Warning.

Clear?

Always. Clarity and accuracy override every other consideration in regulated HCP copy.

Imagery Direction

Abstract scientific illustration over clean white — soft watercolor complement proteins and blood cells in berry-pink and teal-green. Minimal human photography on the HCP home experience.

Do
Keep backgrounds white and uncluttered so dense clinical copy stays legible. Use molecular/cellular imagery to evoke mechanism of action. Any human imagery reads professional and clinical.
Don't
No stock-photo emotional lifestyle imagery on the HCP hero. No imagery that crowds or obscures required safety information. No saturated photographic backgrounds behind body copy.

Content Pillars

Indications

The four FDA-approved uses — NMOSD, gMG, aHUS, PNH — and the patients each serves.

Dosing & Administration

Weight-based dosing, infusion schedules, and long-acting interval guidance for the care team.

Safety & REMS

Logo

Clear space: maintain space around the wordmark equal to the height of the swoosh arc. The white wordmark is built for the teal/navy bar; the colored swoosh provides its own contrast.

  • Don't recolor the swoosh gradient or flatten it to a single hue.
  • Don't place the white wordmark on a light ground without the colored swoosh providing contrast.
  • Don't stretch, rotate, or separate the swoosh from the wordmark.
